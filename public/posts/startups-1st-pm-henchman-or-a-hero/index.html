Startup's 1st PM: Henchman or a Hero?

Nov 20, 2024 · 746 words · 4 minute read

I recently had a chat with the CEO of a small, scrappy, and successful startup. Let’s call him “Mike.”

Mike’s company is pulling in a few million dollars a year with a very lean team. They’ve made it past the “Will we survive?” phase but now face a classic startup dilemma: what to do next. And unfortunately, Mike and his team don’t agree on which persona to prioritize.

Mike wants to build features that help the buyer persona grow their business: loyalty programs, promotions, and tools to bring in more customers. His team, meanwhile, is laser-focused on helping the practitioner persona, advocating for features that automate tedious day-to-day tasks. Both are great ideas. But, as with most startups, there’s only so much time and bandwidth. A choice has to be made.

Mike and I talked about product methodologies for making these trade-offs: Jobs-to-be-Done, RICE, Empathy Mapping—the usual suspects. But the more we talked, the clearer it became: Mike didn’t actually want advice. He wanted a way to justify the decision he’d already made—to prioritize the buyer. When I called him out on it, he hedged: “Well, I want to keep an open mind.”

It’s Not a Democracy 🔗

Mike and his team are caught in a trap of recency and proximity bias. Mike spends most of his time talking to the buyers—selling the product, pitching its value, and envisioning how it could grow their businesses. The team, on the other hand, lives and breathes the practitioner’s pain points. Practitioners are loud. They report bugs, request fixes, and demand better tools for their workflows. Naturally, the team is motivated to make their lives easier.

This disconnect is common. It’s also why a framework like RICE won’t solve the problem. Mike’s team isn’t going to be swayed by a spreadsheet of weighted scores. They don’t need data—they need a compelling story. Something emotionally resonant that aligns their work with the company’s larger vision and gets everyone rowing in the same direction.

After I shared this perspective, Mike disappeared for a while. I couldn’t help but wonder: Did I offend him? Was I too blunt? After all, who am I to tell him what to do with a company I’m not part of, a product I’ve never used, and a team I’ve never met?

Turns out, he was just busy.

Mike’s First PM 🔗

When Mike finally got back to me, not much had changed. But he’d been chewing on our conversation and decided it was time to hire a product manager.

Great move, right? But here’s where I challenged him again—not on hiring a PM (I’m a PM myself, so of course I think it’s a good idea), but on what kind of PM he should hire.

Founders like Mike often fall into the trap of looking for a “strategic” PM—someone to debate high-level trade-offs, dream up roadmaps, and provide fresh perspective. That’s all well and good, but in Mike’s case, I think it’s the wrong call.

Mike doesn’t need a strategist right now. He needs someone great at execution. Someone who can take his vision, translate it for the team, and lead them in that direction.

This kind of PM is more of a translator and diplomat than a visionary. They’re not there to challenge Mike’s opinions—at least not initially. Their job is to amplify Mike’s voice, align the team, and deliver on what’s already in front of them. Over time, sure, they’ll earn the trust and authority to make strategic nudges. But that comes after proving they can execute.

Why Your First PM Isn’t a Visionary 🔗

Many PMs might scoff at the idea of stepping into a role like this. “That’s not real product management,” they’d argue. “That’s just being the founder’s lackey.” But they’re missing the point.

Inheriting a successful product is not about dreaming up something new—it’s about refining what’s already working. It’s about helping the team make smart trade-offs, bringing structure to chaos, and ensuring the wheels of the business keep turning smoothly. Release plans, roadmaps, triaging customer support, running alignment meetings—these are the unsexy, often overlooked fundamentals that early-stage startups desperately need to thrive.

The first PM at Mike’s startup doesn’t need to be a visionary. The team already has plenty of world-changing debates over lunch. What Mike needs is someone who can translate his vision into action, align a scrappy and passionate team around it, and keep the business moving forward—even if it means saying no to some users in the short term.
