The Early PM’s Role: Aligning Vision and Team

Nov 26, 2024 · 668 words · 4 minute read

I recently spoke with the CEO of a scrappy, small, and profitable startup. Let’s call him Mike.

Mike’s company is making a few million dollars a year with a tiny team. They’ve survived the early stages but are now staring at a familiar problem: growth. What’s next? How do they scale? Unfortunately, Mike and his team can’t agree on an answer.

Mike thinks the company should focus on helping their buyers—features like loyalty programs, promotions, and customer acquisition tools. His team, on the other hand, is deeply attached to the practitioners—the ones using the product every day. They want automation, fewer pain points, and fixes for the bugs they hear about constantly.

Both are reasonable strategies, but startups don’t get to do everything. And here’s the truth: Mike already made his decision. He just didn’t want to admit it.

When he asked me if a product management course (like those from the Pragmatic Institute) might help his team prioritize better, I pushed back. He wasn’t looking for clarity; he was looking for validation. Mike wanted a structured way to justify his gut instinct: prioritize the buyer. When I pointed this out, he muttered something about “keeping an open mind.” I didn’t believe him.

It’s Not a Democracy 🔗

Mike’s problem isn’t unique, but it’s deeply frustrating. He and his team have different biases, shaped by who they talk to every day. Mike spends his time with buyers, pitching the product and closing deals. His team hears directly from practitioners—bug reports, feature requests, and endless complaints. It’s natural for each side to advocate for the audience they interact with most.

This kind of disconnect isn’t solved by frameworks or training. Sure, those things can help with decision-making, but Mike’s team doesn’t need another decision tree. They need a story—a cohesive narrative that connects their day-to-day work to the company’s bigger vision. Without it, they’re just spinning their wheels.

This reminded me of my own time as one of the first PMs at a startup. At Checkr, I was the second PM, working under a brilliant but incredibly young director. Our job was to translate the founder’s vision into a roadmap the team could actually execute.

Let me be honest: we weren’t “strategizing.” We were doing what Mike needs his first PM to do—executing the founder’s vision, whether we liked it or not. And that was fine because the company wasn’t ready for anything else. The hardest part wasn’t figuring out what to build; it was keeping the team motivated when their frustrations piled up. As much as I loved the challenge, there were times I felt less like a product manager and more like a peacekeeper.

Mike’s First PM 🔗

When Mike circled back, he’d come to a decision: instead of enrolling his team in a course, he wanted to hire a product manager. A step in the right direction—but I challenged him again.

Mike doesn’t need a “strategic visionary” PM right now. He needs someone who can take his vision, package it for the team, and create a roadmap they can actually follow. This isn’t glamorous work. It’s not about bold product innovation. It’s about being a translator—a diplomat who turns high-level ambition into ground-level clarity.

Would every PM jump at this role? No. It’s not sexy, and it comes with its own frustrations. But having been there myself, I know how critical it is. A good PM in this position doesn’t just “execute orders.” They shape how a company thinks about its product. Over time, they gain enough trust to nudge the strategy and influence decisions.

It’s thankless, unpolished work—somewhere between managing chaos and running damage control. But it’s also the foundation for everything that comes next. Without it, companies like Mike’s stall out, and no amount of vision or talent can save them. It’s not glamorous, but it’s essential.
