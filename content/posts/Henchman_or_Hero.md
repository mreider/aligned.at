I recently had a chat with the CEO of a small, scrappy, and successful startup. I'll call him Mike.

Mike’s company is earning in a few million dollars a year with a very small team. They’ve made it past the “Will we survive?” phase aren't sure what to do next in order to grow the business. And unfortunately, Mike and his team don’t agree on what to prioritize.

Mike wants to build features that help his customer grow their business: loyalty programs, promotions, and tools to bring in more customers. Mike's team, meanwhile, is focused on helping the practitioner, advocating for features that automate tedious day-to-day tasks. Both are fine strategies. But, as with any software company, they can't do everything at once.

Mike doesn't have any product management experience, and there are no PM's at his company. He wondered if a course, like those from the Pragmatic Institute, might help make the decision easier. We debated it a bit. But the more we talked, the clearer it became: Mike didn’t actually want to make the decision clearer. He wanted a way to justify the decision he’d already made — to prioritize the buyer. When I called him out on that, he just hedged: “Well, I want to keep an open mind.” I didn't believe him.

## It’s Not a Democracy

Mike and his team have opposing proximity biases. Mike spends his time talking to the buyers, selling the product,and pitching its value. His team, on the other hand, hears all about the practitioner’s pain points. The practitioners are loud. They report bugs, request fixes, and demand better solutions. Naturally, the team is motivated to make their lives easier.

This disconnect is common. It’s also why frameworks and product management techniques won’t solve the problem. Yes, Mike's team could learn how to make some important decisions based on a solid framework, but in my opinion his team needs more than data. They need an emotionally resonant narrative that aligns their work with the Mike's larger vision and gets everyone rowing in the same direction.

After I shared this perspective, Mike disappeared for a while. I couldn’t help but wonder: Did I offend him? Was I too blunt? After all, who am I to tell him what to do with a company I’m not part of, a product I’ve never used, and a team I’ve never met?

Turns out, he was just busy.

## Mike’s First PM

When Mike finally got back to me, not much had changed. But he’d been chewing on our conversation and decided it might be a good idea to hire a product manager, rather than enroll the team in some sort of product management training.

Like the course idea - I challenged him again. Not about on hiring a PM (I’m a PM myself, so of course I think it’s a good idea), but on what kind of PM he should hire. Mike doesn’t need a strategist right now. He needs someone who can take his vision, translate it for the team, and lead them in that direction.

This kind of PM is more of a diplomat than a visionary. They’re not there to challenge Mike’s opinions — at least not initially. Their job is to align the team, and deliver on what’s already in front of them. Over time, sure, they’ll earn enough trust and authority to make strategic nudges. But that comes after proving they can execute.

Many PMs might scoff at the idea of stepping into a role like this. “That’s just being the founder’s lackey.” I disagree.
